\begin{abstract}
LLM systems are usually compared by mean judge scores, but the operational
risk of a deployment is rarely the average. It is the worst tail. We
extend Causal Judge Evaluation (CJE) from the policy mean to lower-tail
risk by estimating CVaR, the average outcome on the worst $\alpha$ fraction
of responses. The estimator uses the Rockafellar--Uryasev stop-loss form:
calibrate the cheap judge to predict the shortfall $(t-\Y)_+$ instead of
$\Y$, average the calibrated shortfall on target-policy responses, and
maximize over $t$. The audit is the safety mechanism. A two-moment Wald
gate checks two things at the chosen threshold: whether the empirical tail
mass matches $\alpha$, and whether the shortfall calibrator transports to
the target policy. If either check fails, the method refuses to report a
level. At $\alpha = 1$ the audit reduces to the original mean-CJE residual
test, so the same code returns both estimands.
On HealthBench ($n_0 = 500$ prompts, gpt-4o-mini cheap, gpt-4.1 oracle,
$25\%$ oracle slice), CVaR-CJE cuts mean absolute error from $0.204$
(cheap-only) to $0.082$ at $\alpha = 0.10$ across five policies. The
audit catches the one structural failure on the panel, an off-topic
adversarial policy at $\alpha = 0.20$. The cheap-judge calibrator buys
$3.8$--$4.6\times$ variance reduction over a pure-oracle baseline at the
small-slice budget where tail estimation is hardest. The full pipeline
runs in under $30$\,s per policy on a laptop.
\end{abstract}
