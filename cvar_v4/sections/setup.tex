\section{Setup and Estimands}
\label{sec:setup}

For prompt $\X$, policy $\ptarget$ generates response $\A_{\ptarget}$. A cheap judge returns score $\Sscore_{\ptarget}$ and an expensive oracle returns label $\Y_{\ptarget}\in[0,1]$. The oracle may be a human, expert panel, downstream metric, or stronger model. We observe cheap judge scores broadly and oracle labels only on a smaller calibration/audit slice.

The mean value of a policy is
\begin{equation}
V(\ptarget)=\E_{\ptarget}[\Y].
\end{equation}
CJE estimates $V(\ptarget)$ by fitting a calibrated predictor $f(\Sscore,\X)$ of $\Y$ on an oracle slice and averaging it over target-policy responses, subject to a transport audit.

For tail risk, let
\begin{equation}
q_{\alpha}(\ptarget)=\inf\{t:\Pp_{\ptarget}(\Y\leq t)\geq \alpha\}
\end{equation}
be the lower $\alpha$-quantile. When the distribution of $\Y$ is continuous at $q_{\alpha}(\ptarget)$, the lower-tail CVaR is
\begin{equation}
\CVaR_{\alpha}(\ptarget)
=\E_{\ptarget}\!\left[\Y \mid \Y\leq q_{\alpha}(\ptarget)\right].
\label{eq:conditional-cvar}
\end{equation}
Our formal definition is the equivalent optimization form
\begin{equation}
\CVaR_{\alpha}(\ptarget)
=\sup_{t\in\R}\left\{t-\frac{1}{\alpha}\E_{\ptarget}\!\left[\shortfall{t}{\Y}\right]\right\}.
\label{eq:stoploss-cvar}
\end{equation}
The optimizer is an $\alpha$-quantile under standard continuity conditions. This form is the right primitive for estimation because it reduces tail risk to a family of mean shortfall problems.

\paragraph{Discrete outcomes.}
If $\Y$ is a Likert rating or any other atomic outcome, \cref{eq:conditional-cvar} is not the right definition without tie handling. The optimization form in \cref{eq:stoploss-cvar} remains well-defined; the appendix gives the partial-atom version of expected shortfall. The main benchmark therefore targets continuous rubric-style outcomes in $[0,1]$.
